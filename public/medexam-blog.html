How to Score 700+ in Every UCAT Subtest

✍️ Sarah K., Head of Content📅 April 2026⏱ 12 min read

A score of 700+ in each cognitive subtest places you in approximately the top 25% of all UCAT candidates — a position that opens doors at the vast majority of UK medical schools. But achieving this consistently across all four cognitive subtests (VR, DM, QR, and AR) requires a different approach for each one.

This guide breaks down exactly what 700+ requires in each subtest, and the specific strategies that get you there.

💡 Key insight: The UCAT is not an intelligence test. It is a speed-and-accuracy test. The students who score highest are those who have practised the right strategies until they are automatic.

Verbal Reasoning: 700+

The average VR score is around 590–610. To reach 700+, you need to answer at least 35 of 44 questions correctly. The key? Stop reading the whole passage first.

  • Read the question first, then scan the passage — VR is about locating information, not comprehension.
  • For True/False/Can't Tell — if the passage neither confirms nor denies the statement, it's always Can't Tell. Never use outside knowledge.
  • Aim for ~30 seconds per question — any question taking more than 45 seconds should be flagged and skipped.

Decision Making: 700+

DM is the most varied subtest — it covers syllogisms, probability, statistical reasoning, and argument evaluation. The average score is around 610–630.

  • Syllogisms: Always draw a Venn diagram. Don't try to solve them in your head — errors are almost inevitable without a diagram.
  • Probability: Set up the calculation before selecting an answer. Read carefully — questions often give you data in a format that requires converting before calculating.
  • Argument strength: Identify the conclusion, then ask whether the evidence directly supports it or whether there's a gap in the logic.

Quantitative Reasoning: 700+

QR tests numerical problem-solving, not advanced maths. The key skill is reading graphs and tables accurately and calculating quickly.

  • Estimate first — many QR questions can be answered with an estimate. If the answer choices are spread apart, don't calculate precisely.
  • Learn the on-screen calculator — practice using it at speed. Many students lose time fumbling with it during the real exam.
  • Percentage change formula — (new − old) / old × 100. This appears in almost every QR section.

Abstract Reasoning: 700+

AR is the most time-pressured subtest — approximately 14 seconds per question. The students who perform best here have drilled pattern recognition until it's instinctive.

  • Use SCANS: Shape, Colour, Arrangement, Number, Size — check each systematically in 10 seconds. If no pattern is obvious, guess and move on.
  • The most common rules are: number of shapes, number of sides, colour/shading, rotation, and position. Start with these.
  • Daily short practice beats weekly long sessions — 15 minutes every day builds pattern recognition faster than 2 hours once a week.
